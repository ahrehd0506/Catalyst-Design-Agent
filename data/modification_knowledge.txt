TITLE: Exploration Summary Report (Pre-Exploitation)

1) Binding Rule  
- Binding strength is defined as –ΔG; therefore, a more positive ΔG indicates weaker binding, and a more negative ΔG indicates stronger binding.

2) Executive Findings  
- Substitutions in the 2nd shell (notably N for C) consistently produced modest weakening of *O and *OH binding (↑ ΔG) without significant structural distortion (e.g., Iterations 1, 11, 14). However, none fully reached target ΔG ranges.  
- Metal substitutions drastically altered adsorption energies; Co, Ir, Zn, and Cu caused excessive weakening (ΔG too positive), while Cr, Mn, Ta, Mo, and V led to overly strong binding (ΔG too negative), often moving away from target windows (Iterations 3, 8, 15, 30, 34, 24, 43).  
- Coordination shell substitutions showed mixed effects; replacing N with C or O mostly weakened binding moderately, but substituting N with S or H often strengthened binding or destabilized catalyst (e.g., Iterations 2, 7, 16, 35).  
- Functional group addition (e.g., COC, CO) successfully weakened *O and *OH binding (increasing ΔG), moving closer to targets at the cost of stability and higher complexity (Iterations 17, 36).  
- Defect creation generally weakened binding energies but excessively so, leading to too weak binding and increased complexity beyond recommended limits (Iteration 29, 45).  
- Some modifications caused unphysical results, such as negative dissolution potentials (indicative of instability) or excessive overpotential penalties, signaling trade-offs between binding tuning and stability.

3) Modification → Outcome Map  
- 2nd Shell Substitutions with N (e.g., C→N): Produce selective weakening of *O and *OH binding energies; example Iteration 1 (ΔG(*O) 1.49 → 1.63 eV, ΔG(*OH) 0.65 → 0.72 eV), physically reasonable with no distortion.  
- Metal substitutions:  
 • Co, Ir, Zn, Cu cause excessive weakening of adsorbate binding (ΔG *OOH >4.5 eV), overshooting targets (Iterations 3, 15, 30, 34).  
 • Cr, Mn, Ta, Mo, V cause excessive strengthening (ΔG *O and *OH strongly negative), overshooting targets opposite direction with possible stability issues (Iterations 8, 12, 24, 43).  
- Coordination shell substitutions:  
 • Replacing 1st shell N with C weakens binding moderately but insufficiently (Iteration 2).  
 • Replacing N with S or H often strengthens binding or destabilizes catalyst (Iterations 7, 16, 19, 32, 39, 42).  
- Functional group additions such as COC or CO significantly increase ΔG for *O and *OH, moving closer to target but often compromising catalyst stability (Iterations 17, 36).  
- Defect coordination decreases binding energies too strongly and raises complexity, resulting in diminished catalytic efficiency (Iteration 29).  
- Recovery of coordination (e.g., restoring N ligand) reverses excessive weakening/modifies binding toward targets effectively (Iteration 49).

4) Selective Adsorbate-Tuning Patterns  
- 2nd Shell N substitution:  
 • ΔG(*O) ↑ moderately, ΔG(*OH) ↑ moderately, ΔG(*OOH) stable (~) or slight ↑; e.g., Iteration 1, 11.  
- Metal substitution to Co, Ir, Zn, Cu:  
 • Strong ΔG increase (↑) for *OOH, *O, *OH indicating overall weakening (Iteration 3, 15, 30, 34).  
- Metal substitution to Cr, Mn, Ta, Mo, V:  
 • Strong ΔG decrease (↓) for all adsorbates, stronger binding (Iteration 8, 12, 24, 43).  
- Coordination substitution of N with O:  
 • Slight ΔG increase for *O, minimal or inconsistent effect on *OH, slight decrease for *OOH (Iteration 13, 21).  
- Coordination substitution to S or H:  
 • Usually decreases ΔG(*O) and ΔG(*OH) (↓), strengthening binding (Iteration 16, 19, 39, 42).  
- Functional group addition (COC or CO):  
 • ΔG(*O) and ΔG(*OH) significantly ↑, *OOH slightly ↓ or ~ stable (Iterations 17, 36).  
- Defect creation:  
 • Generally decreases ΔG (↓) for all adsorbates, moving binding unexpectedly too weak (Iteration 29, 45).

5) Exploitation Playbook  
- Safe moves:  
 • 2nd shell substitution: C→N to weaken *O and *OH binding moderately without structural distortion (e.g., Iteration 1).  
 • Recovery of lost first-shell N coordination to increase ΔG and tune binding closer to target (Iteration 49).  
 • Functional group addition (CO, COC) for selective *O/*OH tuning with caution on stability (Iterations 17, 36).  
 • Coordination adjustments replacing N with O moderately weakening *O binding (Iterations 13, 21).  
- Conditional moves:  
 • Metal substitution only if the coordination and 2nd shell environment already tunes binding near targets to avoid overshooting (e.g., avoid Co, Ir, Cu when far from targets).  
 • Defect introduction only combined with other tuning to counteract excessive weakening, given complexity and stability risks (Iteration 29, 45).  
 • Second-shell heteroatom swaps between P, S, B to finely probe small ΔG shifts with stability monitoring (Iterations 14, 18, 20).  
- Avoid/guardrails:  
 • Avoid metal substitutions to V, Ta, Mn, Mo and Cu in early exploitation stages due to strong overshooting of binding and catalytic inefficiency (Iterations 24, 27, 30, 34, 43).  
 • Avoid coordination substitutions replacing N with S or H alone as these tend to strengthen binding contrary to targets and may destabilize catalyst (Iterations 16, 19, 39, 42).  
 • Avoid excessive defect creation beyond one site duplication to keep complexity and stability manageable (Iterations 29, 45).  
 • Guard against large complexity increase (>6) which correlates with stability loss and unphysical dissolution potentials.  
- Recurring failure mode:  
 • Metal substitutions frequently cause binding energies to overshoot target ΔG windows strongly in either direction, undermining catalytic performance. Guardrail: restrict metal changes to those near Fe or apply only once binding energies approach target windows.  
 • Coordination substitutions with S or H generally lead to stronger binding (lower ΔG) instead of weakening; require confirming effects on a case-by-case basis, avoiding routine use.  

Summary: The exploration reveals a delicate balance between catalyst coordination, metal identity, and second-shell heteroatom composition that governs adsorbate binding energies. Moderate weakening of *O and *OH binding is most effectively achieved via selective 2nd shell N substitutions and functional group addition, while metal swaps often induce excessive and detrimental shifts. Exploitation should leverage these safer, consistent tuning knobs initially, monitor complexity and stability metrics closely, and use metal or defect modifications conditionally with strict guardrails to prevent unphysical or overly complex structures.

- Coordination defects partially tune binding energies but tend to overshoot *O binding strength, suggesting careful defect control is needed.

  • Generally moves *O and *OH closer to target but risks overshooting *OOH.

  • Slightly increases ΔG(*O) and ΔG(*OH), moderate effect on *OOH, improving overpotential (↑ *O, ↑ *OH, ~ *OOH); Iter #17, #45.  
- Ligand O addition:  
  • Strongly weakens all bindings with large ΔG increases (↑ *O, ↑ *OH, ↑ *OOH), overshoot beyond target; Iter #15.

  - Axial or ligand addition only if other tuning options exhausted, due to risk of overshooting and increased overpotential (Iter #15).  
  - Metal substitution to Mo or Ni only if aiming to explore extremes in binding strength for experimental contrast, with caution due to poor target alignment and stability.  
  - Apply coordination changes (e.g., N→C in first shell) if electronic effects suggest possible fine-tuning, monitoring for unphysical binding strength shifts (Iter #28, #47).  
- Avoid/guardrails:  
  - Avoid second-shell P substitution in Ru or Co SACs as it tends to induce unphysical binding site shifts and inconsistent *O binding (Iter #9, #33, #22, #29, #42, #46).  
  - Avoid metal substitutions to Mo in this coordination environment due to excessive overbinding and poor catalytic metrics (Iter #13, #23, #34).  
  - Avoid Ni substitution except for exploration of extreme binding weakening given poor overlap with target adsorption energies and high overpotential (Iter #4, #44).

  - Guardrail: Always verify adsorption site and binding site consistency post-modification to ensure physical relevance and avoid unphysical adsorption behavior.  
  - Monitor complexity increases above 5 as they can hinder efficient exploration and may correspond to structural instability.

This summary distills critical modification effects on adsorption energetics, stability, and complexity, enabling accelerated exploitation of SAC design space with minimized risk and guided tuning of ORR intermediates binding strength.

1) Binding Rule  
- Binding_Strength is defined as – ΔG; thus, more positive ΔG indicates weaker binding, and more negative ΔG indicates stronger binding.

2) Executive Findings  
- Metal substitution strongly modulates all adsorption energies (*OOH, *O, *OH), often overshooting the target windows; Ir consistently achieves the best balance among tested metals.  
- First-shell coordination substitutions (especially O↔S, N↔C, and H↔O/H) enable moderate tuning of adsorption energies, with some success in strengthening/weaking specific adsorbate bindings, but trade-offs in dissolution potential and overpotential arise.  
- Second-shell heteroatom doping (B, P, S, N) influences adsorption energies, but effects are complex and can cause unbalanced shifts or stability loss; boron doping generally weakens binding energies, while phosphorus often over-strengthens *O binding.  
- Ligand addition (OH, O) selectively strengthens *OOH binding but frequently over-strengthens *O and *OH bindings, complicating the optimization of adsorption balance.  
- Coordination substitutions from O to S often unexpectedly strengthen *O and *OH binding (more negative ΔG), contrary to typical electronegativity assumptions, highlighting the subtle electronic effects in SAC environments.  
- The best adsorption energy balance and catalytic performance metrics (lowest overpotential ~0.39–0.48 V and high dissolution potential >1.0 V) were achieved with Ir metal, mixed first-shell coordination of C, N, H, and carefully tuned second-shell doping (primarily C and B), and optional axial O ligand.

3) Modification → Outcome Map  
- Metal substitution:  
  • Ir shifts *O and *OH closer to target ranges though *OOH often weakens, increasing overpotential (e.g., iters 11, 16, 18).  
  • Pd substitution consistently over-weakens binding for all adsorbates and changes adsorption sites, causing unphysical shifts (iters 16, 25, 38).  
  • Mo strongly over-strengthens binding (too negative ΔG), leading to very poor catalytic metrics (iters 14, 29).  
  • Ta causes *OOH to weaken excessively and *O binding to strengthen excessively, leading to poor balance (iters 37, 50).  
  
- First-shell coordination substitutions:  
  • O→S often strengthens *O and *OH binding despite lower electronegativity of S; weakens *OOH slightly (iters 7, 47, 41).  
  • N→C weakens *OH binding and generally adjusts *O moderately (iters 32, 43).  
  • H substitutions for O or N weaken binding energies moderately but may reduce catalyst stability (iters 13, 35, 44).  
  • C substitutions in first shell produce mixed but mostly moderate tuning effects on *O and *OH (iters 10, 27, 43).  
  
- Second-shell doping:  
  • B doping generally weakens all adsorbates’ binding energies, sometimes overshooting for *OOH (iters 9, 15, 31, 34, 46, 49).  
  • P doping overly strengthens *O but weakens *OOH binding resulting in poor balance (iters 6, 12, 26, 33, 48).  
  • S doping effects vary, often weakening *OOH while strengthening *O/*OH unexpectedly (iters 4, 20, 36, 40).  
  • N doping slightly increased adsorption energies but caused drop in dissolution potential (iter 42).  
  
- Ligand modifications:  
  • OH ligand consistently strengthens *OOH binding to target range but over-strengthens *O and *OH binding, raising overpotential and complexity (iters 23, 24).  
  • O ligand tuning shows promise in balancing adsorption (iters 45, 46).  
  
- Physically reasonable modifications: all substitutions and ligand additions maintained atomic and structural integrity, no distortions visible. Unphysical outcomes usually arise from electronic structure changes or shifts in adsorption sites rather than structural distortions.

4) Selective Adsorbate-Tuning Patterns  
- “Metal Ir substitution”:  
  *OOH ΔG: ↓ (strengthens), *O ΔG: ↑/↓ (moderate), *OH ΔG: ↑/↓ (moderate). Example: Iter 11, *OOH=4.57 eV, *O=1.77 eV, *OH=1.05 eV.   
- “First-shell coordination O→S”:  
  *OOH ΔG: ↓ slightly, *O ΔG: ↓ (strengthened binding), *OH ΔG: ↓ (stronger binding). Iter 47: *OOH 3.67 eV, *O 1.94 eV, *OH 0.5 eV.  
- “Second-shell B doping”:  
  All ΔG ↑ (weaker binding), *OOH often overshoots active window. Iter 34: *OOH 4.45 eV, *O 2.26 eV, *OH 1.22 eV.  
- “Second-shell P doping”:  
  *O ΔG ↓ strongly (over-strengthening), *OOH and *OH variable. Iter 12: *OOH 4.77 eV, *O -0.26 eV (excessively strong).  
- “Adding OH ligand”:  
  Keeps *O and *OH ΔG low (strong binding), shifts *OOH ΔG down toward target. Iter 23: *OOH 3.66 eV, *O 1.79 eV, *OH 0.38 eV.  
- “First-shell H substitution”:  
  Raises ΔG of all adsorbates (weakens binding), modest effect. Iter 44: *OOH 3.99 eV, *O 2.03 eV, *OH 0.66 eV.  
- “Second-shell S doping”:  
  Slightly lowers *OOH ΔG, but often strengthens *O and weakens *OH unexpectedly. Iter 36: *OOH 3.81 eV, *O 1.95 eV, *OH 0.55 eV.

5) Exploitation Playbook  
- Safe moves:  
  • Start with Ir metal centered catalysts; maintains best overall adsorption balance and stability.  
  • First-shell coordination tuning: C, N, and occasional H substitutions for fine-tuning *O and *OH ΔG near targets (e.g., iters 32, 43, 44).  
  • Second-shell boron doping to moderately weaken bindings and improve dissolution potential, but monitor *OOH overshoot (iters 15, 31, 46).  
  • Use axial O ligands to improve *OOH binding selectively (iters 45, 46), balancing with noticeable complexity increase.  
  • Maintain coordination with ‘N’ atoms in first shell; substitutions avoiding too many N→C or N→H changes at once.  
  • Moderate use of sulfur in coordination or 2nd shell can tune *OOH, but requires careful monitoring of *O and *OH.  
  
- Conditional moves:  
  • P doping in second shell only with strict monitoring of *O overbinding and dissolution potential (IPs 6, 12, 33, 48 negative results).  
  • S substitutions in first shell to weaken *OOH but try to prevent over-strengthening *O/*OH possibly by combining with other first-shell substitutions (mixed results at 7, 41, 47).  
  • Metal substitutions to Ru or Mo for *OOH improvement only if *O and *OH strongly bind too much and high complexity allowed (Ru often overbinds *O/*OH; Mo leads to extreme results).  
  • Increase complexity carefully when simultaneous multiple substitutions are done; avoid if stability metrics degrade sharply.  
  
- Avoid/guardrails:  
  • Pd substitution consistently leads to excessive weakening of adsorbate binding and site instability; avoid (iters 16, 25, 38).  
  • Mo substitution causes excessively strong binding resulting in poor catalytic activity and stability; avoid for fine tuning (iters 14, 29).  
  • Phosphorus doping in second shell destabilizes balance with too strong *O binding and drastically increased overpotential; avoid or use very cautiously (its 12, 26, 33, 48).  
  • Over substitution in first shell (e.g., multiple H or C simultaneously) can lower dissolution potential substantially; guard with stability checks (e.g., iters 13, 44).  
  • Avoid substituting first-shell S for H without compensating changes, as it may sharply decrease dissolution potential (iter 35).  
  • Be cautious of coordination substitutions that shift binding sites from top to bridge or vice versa, as they may introduce unanticipated electronic effects and unbalanced adsorption energies.  
  
- Recurring failure mode:  
  Electronic overcompensation from second-shell heteroatom doping (especially P and Pd substitutions) causes unbalanced adsorption energies, either by excessive strengthening or weakening. Guardrail: prioritize substitution steps with accompanied adsorption energy monitoring and revert to most balanced recent catalysts if overcorrection detected.  
  
Overall, exploitation should focus on moderate stepwise substitutions around Ir metal center with first-shell mixed coordination chemistry and second-shell B and C doping, combined with axial ligands like O/OH, to finely tune adsorption energies within target windows while preserving catalyst stability and manageable complexity.
